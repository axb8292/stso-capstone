How can the aquarium hobby advocate for the animals and the environment?

If you want fish or someone you know wants fish…

Do some research! Every fish has different care requirements and needs. The betta splendens and ancistrus cirrhosus require care that is different. Depending on your daily routine, there is a correct fish species for you. YouTube and online forums are great sources of information! Some great resources are linked below.

Buy local! This is the best thing you can do for the animal and the environment. Local breeders put a lot of care in raising happy and healthy animals that then do not need to be shipped in from a different country. These consist of breeders in the hobby or small local aquarium stores. Your local big box store will get their animals shipped in as imports. The Rochester area has many hobby breeders as well as a great locally owned store called “Rock Beach Aquatics.” To buy from local hobbiests, the r/aquaswap reddit has been a good experience to work with.

Consider the responsibility! All too often, fish are an impulse buy. Like a dog or a cat however, these animals can live a long time, some like ancistrus cirrhosus will live easily 10 years. Owning a fish is a commitment and a life to take care of, not a disposable decoration. If you find yourself or someone you know unable to care for their pet, there are many rescues online and in the community that will take in the unwanted animal(s). Do not flush them down the toilet or release them into a body of water!

Dispose deceased pets with respect! When your fish dies, an all too common response is to flush it down the toilet. This is not only disrespectful to the animal, but also detrimental to the local water supply. When you flush a dead animal, whatever diseases or parasites that caused the animal to die. By burying the fish or putting it in the general trash or compost, the waterways remain safe.

If you do not want fish…

Advocate for policy makers to take the fish trade seriously! Become aware of your local branches of the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, and contact them about the environmental problems caused by irresponsible releases of aquarium fish into local waterways.

If you see a fish in poor living conditions, speak up! In many offices or classrooms, there are fish being kept in small dirty bowls. Educating others is the first step into improving the lives of pet fish.
